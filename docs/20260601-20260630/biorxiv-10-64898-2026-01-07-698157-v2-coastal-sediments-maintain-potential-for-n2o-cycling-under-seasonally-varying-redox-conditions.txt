Abstract 26 

27 

Coastal eutrophication  can lead to  deoxygenation  and  sulfide accumulation  in sediments ,28 

which  could  strongly  impact  the  dynamics  of the  potent greenhouse gas  nitrous oxide (N 2O).29 

Here, we investigated  the effects of oxygen (O 2) and sulfide on  microbial  N2O production 30 

and consumption  in  surface sediments  of a seasonally  euxinic  (anoxic and sulfidic)  coastal 31 

basin . In spring, the se  surface sediments are oxygenated, while  they are highly sulfidic  during 32 

stratification of the water column in summer . During  oxygenated spring conditions , rapid 33 

depletion of  the substrates  O2 and nitrate (NO 3-) in the  surface  sediment  limited  net  in  situ 34 

N2O production,  despite potential for nitrification and for N 2O production through incomplete 35 

denitrification  as observed in batch incubations . Based on metagenome and 36 

metatranscriptome analyses the N 2O-consuming microbial community was  shown to be 37 

highly diverse and dominated by clade II  nosZ -possessing  Flavobacteriia . Assessing  the 38 

summer  sulfidic  conditions  in surface sediments  via batch incubations, we found that 39 

moderate sulfide concentrations (0.2 –1 mM) enhanced  N2O consumption, whereas high 40 

concentrations (4 mM) inhibited  all steps of  denitrification . These findings  highlight the 41 

redox controls on N 2O dynamics , by indicating  that  coastal  sediments  can  maintain 42 

significant  N2O turnover potential despite substrate limitations and elevated sulfide 43 

concentrations . Consequently, ecosystem restoration strategies that alter  O2, NO 3- and sulfide 44 

availability may fundamentally  impact  coastal  N2O budgets. 45

Introduction 46 

47 

Nitrous oxide (N 2O)  is a highly potent greenhouse gas with a centennial atmospheric lifetime ,48 

and  a primary ozone -depleting substance  (Prather et al., 2015; Ravishankara et al., 2009) .49 

Concerningly, g lobal  N2O emissions have risen by 10% since 1980 , with significant 50 

emissions from marine  ecosystems  (Tian et al., 2024) . Due to their proximity to land, c oastal 51 

systems  are very  active  in terms of biogeochemical cycling , which  in turn  also makes them 52 

vulnerable to anthropogenic pressures , such as eutrophication via increased nitrogen loading 53 

(Kuliński et al., 2022) . Eutrophication and warming -induced stratification are driving 54 

widespread deoxygenation  in coastal systems  (Breitburg et al., 2018) , leading to  anoxic 55 

surface  sediments  characterized by  the  accumulation of reduced compounds,  such as 56 

ammonium  (NH 4+) and hydrogen sulfide  (H 2S)  (Jäntti & Hietanen, 2012; Żygadłowska et al., 57 

2023) . Through the tight link between elemental cycles, such altered redox conditions c ould 58 

severely impact N 2O dynamics in coastal  sediments  (Murray et al., 2015) . However, the 59 

effect s of  ongoing deoxygenation and the expansion of  sulfidic  conditions  on N 2O dynamics 60 

are complex and not yet well -understood , contributing to the high uncertainty  in the marine 61 

N2O budget  (Bange et al., 2019; Resplandy et al., 2024) .62 

63 

Net  N2O dynamics  are  dictated by the balance between production and consumption 64 

pathways.  While  N2O can be formed abiotically  via metals, organic material or sunlight 65 

(Leon -Palmero et al., 2025; Otte et al., 2019; Zhu -Barker et al., 2015) , biotic  pr oduction 66 

occurs primarily through  microbial  nitrification and denitrification.  Under oxygen (O 2)67 

limitation , ammonia -oxidizing bacteria ( AOB ) generate  N2O as a byproduct of  the aerobic 68 

oxidation  of the hydroxylamine intermediate  or via nitrifier -denitrification  (Caranto et al., 69 

2016; Kozlowski et al., 2014; Zhu et al., 2013) , while  ammonia -oxidizing  archaea (AOA) 70

produce N 2O from NH 4+ and  nitrite ( NO 2-) through a hybrid mechanism  (Kozlowski et al., 71 

2016; Stieglmeier et al., 2014) . In anoxic zones, denitrification , the stepwise reduction of 72 

nitrate (NO 3−) to dinitrogen (N 2), can release  N2O. This process is generally heterotrophic, 73 

coupled to organic matter degradation, though chemolithotrophic reduction can also occur 74 

(Bruckner et al., 2013; Galán et al., 2014) . Conversely, the only known biological sink 75 

for  N2O is its reduction to  N2 by  microorganisms  possessing  the enzyme nitrous oxide 76 

reductase (NosZ ). Genomic analyses distinguish between microorganisms carrying clade 77 

I nosZ , typically associated  with complete denitrifiers, and those carrying clade II  nosZ ,78 

which often lack upstream denitrification genes but may exhibit a higher affinity for  N2O79 

(Hallin et al., 2018; Yoon et al., 2025) . Clade II -bearing taxa may  therefore  represent a 80 

specialized N 2O sink community capable of scavenging  N2O at low concentration s (Laureni 81 

et al., 2025) . Recently,  a third , lactonase -related  NosZ clade,  clade III , has been  identified 82 

(He et al., 2025) , but its contribution to N 2O removal  has  not  yet been  well explored. 83 

84 

In  eutrophic  coastal  systems  porewater sulfide can reach  mM -range  concentrations  (Dalcin 85 

Martins et al., 2024; Żygadłowska et al., 2023)  as a result of  deoxygenation  and  intense 86 

sulfate  (SO 42-) reduction  via  organic matter mineralization . Sulfide  can severely impact  N2O87 

dynamics by interacting with  the nitrogen cycle. It inhibits nitrification  (Æsøy et al., 1998; 88 

Bejarano Ortiz et al., 2013; Joye & Hollibaugh, 1995)  and, crucially, can  halt  denitrification ,89 

particularly  at  the  N2O reduction  step , leading to N 2O accumulation  (Dalsgaard et al., 2014; 90 

Li et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2013; Senga et al., 2006) . Sulfide can  also inhibit  N2O production 91 

from denitrification  indirectly by  stimulat ing  dissimilatory nitrate reduction to  ammoni um 92 

(DNRA)  (An & Gardner, 2002; Brunet & Garcia -Gil, 1996; Murphy et al., 2020) , which 93 

competes  with denitrification  for the available  NO 3-. However, sulfide may  also  stimulate 94 

denitrification by serving as an electron donor for autotrophic denitrifiers  (Shao et al., 2010) .95

Consequently, the net impact of sulfide on coastal  N2O dynamics , whether it acts primarily as 96 

an inhibitor  or substrate  of  denitrification , remains poorly constrained, particularly in systems 97 

undergoing seasonal redox oscillations. 98 

99 

Here, we  explored the  effects  of O 2 and sulfide  on N 2O cycling  in the surface sediments of a 100 

seasonally euxinic  (anoxic and sulfidic)  coastal basin ( marine Lake Grevelingen,  The 101 

Netherlands ).  At this site,  bottom waters and surface sediments are oxygenated in winter  and 102 

early spring , whereas  stratification leads to  euxinic  bottom waters  and highly sulfidic 103 

sediments  in summer,  creating distinct  seasonal  variation s in redox conditions  (Żygadłowska 104 

et al., 2023) . We  expected  strong  effects of  these redox  variations  on the microbial  N2O105 

dynamics.  We  combined  oxic, anoxic and sulfidic  batch  incubations with microsensor 106 

profiling  and  porewater , metagenom e and  metatranscriptome  analys es to: (i)  assess  the 107 

potential for N 2O production and consumption  in  surface sediments  under oxic and anoxic 108 

conditions , (ii) identify the active microbial taxa driving these  processes , and (iii)  assess  the 109 

effect  of sulfide  on  N2O production and consumption through denitrification . Our findings 110 

highlight critical  redox -associated  mechanisms  that regulate  N2O dynamics  in eutrophic 111 

coastal sediments , thereby  necessitating a nuanced understanding of sediment redox 112 

chemistry in future climate mitigation strategies .113

Materials and methods 114

Study area 116 

Lake Grevelingen is a former estuary located in the southwest of The Netherlands. It was 117 

dammed on its landward and seaward sides in 1964 and 1971, respectively. Since 1978, 118 

exchange of water with the North Sea occurs via an underwater opening, hence the salinity of 119 

Lake Grevelingen is  currently comparable to that of the adjacent marine waters (~29 -32)  (e.g. 120 

7, 73) . Lake Grevelingen has a surface area of 115 km 2 and an average depth of 5.1 m, 121 

however, deeper former estuarine tidal channels are still present. The nutrient status of Lake 122 

Grevelingen is eutrophic. 123 

124 

This study focuses on Scharendijke basin (51.742° N, 3.849° E), a 45 -m deep basin within 125 

one of the former estuarine tidal channels. The basin is characterized by a high sediment 126 

accumulation rate (~13 -20 cm yr -1) and a high flux of organic matter to the sediment -water 127 

interface (~91 mol C m -2 yr -1) (Egger et al., 2016; Van Helmond et al., 2025) . Temperature -128 

induced stratification in summer leads to euxinic bottom waters from approximately June to 129 

September, while in spring the water column is well -mixed with oxygenated bottom waters 130 

(Żygadłowska et al., 2023) . Summer stratification and the absence of O 2 in bottom waters 131 

lead to accumulation of reduced compounds , such as  NH 4+ and H 2S. In underlying surface 132 

sediments, NH 4+ concentrations are high year -round, while concentrations of H 2S are  only 133 

high in summer  (Żygadłowska et al., 2023) .134

Microsensor depth profiling 164

H2S concentrations in the sediment  interfere with  the microsensors. Microsensor tip sizes 167 

were 50 μm for O 2 and H 2S, and 500 μm for N 2O. Depth profiles  were  determined  in 168 

sediment cores as soon as possible after collection (typically all profiles were measured 169

Potential for N 2O-mediating processes 312 

To identify the processes that  could  contribute to N 2O production  and consumption  in the 313 

surface sediments  of  Scharendijke  basin , batch incubations were set up  under both oxic and 314 

anoxic conditions . In this way we could link the potential  to the seasonally changing  redox 315

Discussion 475 

476 

The prevailing redox regime in coastal sediments can strongly impact N 2O cycling by 477 

stimulating or inhibiting processes that can lead to net N 2O production and consumption 478 

(Cheung et al., 2025; Hylén et al., 2022; Jameson et al., 2021) . In  eutrophic  and  seasonally 479 

stratified  coastal  systems like the Scharendijke basin  studied here , these  redox conditions  are 480 

dictated by seasonal  changes in  O2 availability  and sulfide accumulation  (Żygadłowska et al., 481 

2023) . We therefore combined geochemical profiling , batch incubations and metagenome and 482 

metatranscriptome analyses  to evaluate  the  effects of O 2 and sulfide  on  microbial  N2O483 

dynamics in surface sediments . The i ncubations  were performed  to study N 2O formation and 484 

consumption potential  and to link this potential to the seasonally changing geochemical 485 

conditions of the surface  sediments  in  Scharendijke  basin , rather than  to  determine  in 486 

situ  rates.  Combined,  we  demonstrate  sedimentary  potential for  microbial  N2O cycling  and 487 

accumulation  through denitrification , which  is  strongly modulated by substrate  availability 488 

(O 2, NO 3-) and sulfide  concentration .489 

490 

Sedimentary N 2O dynamics  during  oxygenated bottom  water conditions  in spring 491 

Despite oxygenated bottom waters,  the  surface sediments  of Scharendijke basin  showed  net 492 

N2O consumption  in  spring , likely driven by a specialized community of clade II  nosZ -493 

carrying  Flavobacteriia  that effectively reduce N 2O, similar to brackish and permeable 494 

sediments  (Marchant et al., 2018; Nakagawa et al., 2019; Nguyen -Dinh et al., 2025) .495 

However, our batch incubations and metagenome and metatranscriptome analyses revealed 496 

that  the  lack of net N 2O production  is not due to  deficient  production  capability,  but rather 497 

substrate limitation. Indeed, O 2 and NO 3-, substrates for the processes of nitrification and 498 

denitrification which can  both  lead to N 2O production, were rapidly depleted within the top 499

Our results re veal a dual  effect of sulfide  on denitrification , as sulfide  acts  as  a stimulant  at 528 

moderate concentrations  (0.2 -1 mM)  and as  an  inhibitor  at high concentrations  (4 mM) . At 529 

concentrations u p to  1 mM,  sulfide enhanced the consumption of  N2O,  likely by  serving as 530 

electron donor for  autotrophic denitrification  (Cardoso et al., 2006; Shao et al., 2010; Wang 531 

et al., 2026) , also  reflected by the  microbial community’s  metagenomic  potential for sulfur 532 

oxidation . However,  at 4 mM sulfide , an environmentally relevant concentration at 533 

Scharendijke basin  (Klomp et al., 2025; Żygadłowska et al., 2023) , we  no longer  observed 534 

N2O production and consumption.  This indicates broad metabolic inhibition,  for example due 535 

to  the immobilization of metal cofactors in key enzymes like NosZ and nitrate reductase  (Fu 536 

et al., 2018; Manconi et al., 2006) . Moreover,  the  enhanced formation of NH 4+ at 1 mM 537 

sulfide compared to 0 and 0.2 mM sulfide suggests an additional role of DNRA  in the 538 

presence of sulfide.  DNRA -performing microorganisms are indeed considered more resistant 539 

to sulfide than denitrifiers  (Caffrey et al., 2019) , and s timulation of DNRA  in the presence of 540 

sulfide  indirect ly  inhi bits  N2O production through denitrification  via  competition  for NO x541 

(An & Gardner, 2002; Brunet & Garcia -Gil, 1996; Murphy et al., 2020) . Although these 542 

findings suggest that the onset of euxinia does not immediately collapse the 543 

sediment’s  capacity for N 2O cycling, limitation of O 2 and  NO 3- required for N 2O production 544 

from nitrification and denitrification is even more severe in summer  (Klomp et al., 2025; Sun 545 

et al., 2026; Żygadłowska et al., 2023) . Consequently,  during peak sulfidic conditions, 546 

sedimentary N 2O cycling could be halted entirely .547

Implications  for N 2O emissions from  coastal  eutrophic sediments 549 

> (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission. The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 15, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.01.07.698157 doi: bioRxiv preprint

Combined,  we  show that coastal sedimentary N 2O dynamics strongly depend on substrate 550 

availability  and sulfide concentrations , hi ghlighting the  redox -associated mechanisms  that 551 

regulate  N2O dynamics  in eutrophic coastal sediments . Paradoxically,  sediments  at 552 

oligotrophic  (Jameson et al., 2024; Maher et al., 2016)  as well as highly eutrophic sites ( this 553 

study;  Foster & Fulweiler, 2016)  are characterized by net N 2O consumption  due to the 554 

limited availability of  inorganic nitrogen  in the form of NO x. In fact, s edimentary N 2O555 

production is expected to peak at low O₂ and high NO x concentrations  (Murray et al., 2015) 556 

as indicated by enhanced N 2O production upon  O2 limitation  (Jameson et al., 2021)  and 557 

substrate addition  (Jameson et al., 2024; Meyer et al., 2008) . Concomitantly,  reoxygenation 558 

of anoxic sediments  in the eutrophic Baltic Sea  stimulated  sedimentary  N2O production 559 

(Hylén et al., 2022) . Yet,  the  net impact  of the sedimentary  N2O dynamics  on sediment -water 560 

N2O fluxes  not only  depends on  substrate availability but also  on  the metabolic capacity of 561 

the endogenous microbial community and  the depth at which N 2O production and 562 

consumption occur  (Meyer et al., 2008) . Nonetheless,  these combined  findings suggest that 563 

the restoration of highly eutrophic systems will  (temporarily) stimulate sedimentary N 2O564 

production,  which could impact the coastal  N2O budget .565
